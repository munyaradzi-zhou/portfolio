Building FarmMonitor: The Problem Nobody Talks About

FarmMonitor Series · Part 1 of 4

This is Part 1 of a 4-part series on building FarmMonitor, an AI-powered platform that helps Zimbabwean farmers get the most out of their extension officer visits. No tech jargon required — if you've ever forgotten your doctor's instructions by the time you got home, you'll understand exactly what we're solving.

The Doctor's Office Problem

Picture this: You visit your doctor. She spends 20 minutes explaining your condition, recommending lifestyle changes, prescribing medication, and telling you what to watch out for. You nod along, feeling confident.

By the time you reach the parking lot, you've already forgotten half of what she said.

Sound familiar? Studies show patients forget 40–80% of medical information immediately after a visit. That's why apps like postvisit.ai exist — they help patients review, understand, and act on their doctor's advice after they leave the clinic.

Now imagine the same problem, but the stakes are a family's food supply for the entire year.

Meet Zimbabwe's Extension Officers

In Zimbabwe (and across much of sub-Saharan Africa), agricultural extension officers are the "doctors" for farmers. They're government-employed experts who travel from farm to farm, advising smallholder farmers on everything from soil health to pest control to climate adaptation.

Think of them as agricultural consultants who make house calls — except each officer might be responsible for hundreds of farmers spread across vast rural areas. They visit each farm maybe once every few months, spend an hour or two observing crops, checking livestock, and dispensing advice.

And just like the doctor's office, the moment the officer drives away, the advice starts fading.

"Plant your maize by the second week of November, apply Compound D fertilizer at 300kg per hectare, watch for fall armyworm — if you see window-paning on the leaves, apply Ampligo within 48 hours."

That's a real recommendation. Now imagine hearing ten of those in one visit, and your next follow-up isn't for two months.

Why This Matters More Than You Think

For a smallholder farmer in rural Zimbabwe, a missed recommendation isn't just an inconvenience — it can mean:

  • Planting too late and missing the rainy season entirely (no harvest)
  • Using the wrong fertilizer ratio and burning your crops
  • Ignoring early pest signs until half the field is destroyed
  • Missing a vaccination window for livestock and losing cattle to disease

These aren't hypothetical. They happen every growing season. And the extension officers? They're stretched thin. The average officer-to-farmer ratio in Zimbabwe can be as high as 1:800. They physically cannot follow up with every farmer between visits.

The "Aha" Moment

The idea for FarmMonitor came from a simple question: If postvisit.ai can help patients remember and act on medical advice, why can't we do the same for farmers?

What postvisit.ai does What FarmMonitor does
Records the doctor's visit notes Records the officer's farm observations
Sends patients a summary they can review Sends farmers a visit summary via app + Telegram
Lets patients ask questions about their treatment Lets farmers ask questions about agricultural advice
Tracks if patients follow treatment plans Tracks if farmers follow recommendations
Reminds patients about medications Reminds farmers about seasonal tasks

The parallel is almost one-to-one. But farming in rural Zimbabwe comes with unique challenges that a medical app never has to deal with:

  1. Connectivity — Many farmers don't have consistent internet access. Some don't have smartphones at all.
  2. Language — Official advice is usually in English, but many farmers are more comfortable in Shona or Ndebele.
  3. Literacy — Some farmers prefer voice or pictures over text.
  4. Cost — Farmers can't afford expensive software subscriptions.

What We Set Out to Build

FarmMonitor needed to be more than a note-taking app. It needed to be an entire support system that works even in the most challenging conditions. Here's what we envisioned:

For the Extension Officer:

  • A dashboard showing all their assigned farmers
  • A simple way to log farm visits with observations and recommendations
  • AI that helps draft structured recommendations from raw notes
  • Risk scores showing which farmers need urgent attention
  • Analytics showing whether their advice is actually being followed

For the Farmer:

  • A personal dashboard with weather, crop status, and pending tasks
  • Visit summaries they can review anytime
  • An AI assistant that explains complex advice in simple language
  • Telegram bot access for farmers who prefer messaging apps
  • SMS notifications for farmers without smartphones
  • Input cost tracking with profit/loss calculations

The key insight: The platform doesn't replace the extension officer — it amplifies them. One officer can now effectively support their entire farmer base between visits, because the system handles the follow-up, reminders, and Q&A that they simply don't have time for.

The Demo: Making It Real

To prove the concept, we built a fully functional demo with real (but fictional) users:

  • 3 extension officers — John Smith, Rumbidzai Shoko, Amos Dube
  • 10 farmers — ranging from livestock keepers to maize/soybean farmers to tobacco growers
  • 23 farm visits with detailed observations
  • 45 recommendations at various stages of completion
  • 22 crop records with actual yield data
  • Profile photos for every user (because real products feel real)

Each farmer even has a name that's a Shona word for an animal — Tsuro (hare), Shumba (lion), Mbizi (zebra). A small touch, but it grounds the demo in Zimbabwean culture.
