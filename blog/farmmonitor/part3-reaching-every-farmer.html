Building FarmMonitor: Reaching Every Farmer

FarmMonitor Series · Part 3 of 4

This is Part 3 of a 4-part series. In Part 2, we explored how AI powers the chat, photo diagnosis, and recommendation engine. Now let's solve the hardest problem in agricultural technology: actually reaching the farmer.

The Connectivity Lie

Here's a dirty secret about "tech for good" projects in developing countries: most of them assume the user has a smartphone with a stable internet connection, a modern browser, and enough data to load a web app.

In rural Zimbabwe, that assumption fails. Hard.

  • Internet coverage is patchy outside major towns. A farmer in Chipinge might get 2G signal on a good day.
  • Smartphones are common but not universal. Many farmers still use basic feature phones.
  • Data costs are real. Loading a full web application can cost more than a farmer earns in a day.
  • Electricity is intermittent. Phones get charged at the local shops or from car batteries.

So building a beautiful React web app and calling it a day would be like building a hospital and putting it on top of a mountain. Great facility — nobody can get there.

FarmMonitor needed to meet farmers where they actually are. That meant three communication channels, each designed for a different level of connectivity.

Channel 1: The Web App (For When Things Work)

The full FarmMonitor web interface is the richest experience — dashboards, charts, crop records, AI chat with photo upload, weather forecasts, the works. It's designed mobile-first (big buttons, clean layout, minimal scrolling) because officers use tablets in the field and farmers use phone browsers.

But the web app is the premium channel. It works great in town, at the agricultural office, or when the farmer has WiFi. For everything else, there's Telegram.

Channel 2: Telegram Bots (The Sweet Spot)

Here's why Telegram is the sweet spot for Zimbabwean farmers:

  1. Low data usage — Telegram messages use a fraction of what a web app consumes
  2. Works on basic smartphones — Runs on Android 4.1+, which covers most phones in circulation
  3. Offline-friendly — Messages queue up when offline and send when signal returns
  4. Already popular — Many Zimbabwean farmers already use Telegram for community groups
  5. Free — No subscription, no API costs for the user

Each Farmer Gets Their Own Bot

This is the part that usually surprises people. In FarmMonitor, each farmer doesn't share a single bot — they get a personal Telegram bot. It's like having a private hotline to their own AI agricultural advisor.

Here's what a farmer's Telegram conversation looks like:

Farmer opens @munashembizi_bot on Telegram

Bot: Welcome to FarmMonitor, Munashe!

I'm your agricultural assistant. You can:
/recommendations - View your active action items
/myrecords       - See your farm records
/crops           - View your current crops
/inputs          - View recent input costs
/help            - See all commands

Or just type a question and I'll help!

The farmer can then check their recommendations:

Farmer: /recommendations

Bot: Your Active Recommendations:

HIGH  Apply fall armyworm treatment immediately
      Spray Ampligo on affected eastern section at 0.3L/ha
      Status: pending | Due: 2025-12-15

MED   Top-dress maize with AN fertilizer
      Apply at 200kg/ha when plants reach knee height
      Status: in_progress | Due: 2025-12-20

LOW   Consider drip irrigation for vegetable garden
      Would reduce water usage by 40%
      Status: pending

Or ask a free-text question:

Farmer: When should I harvest my soybeans?

Bot: Based on your records, you planted soybeans in October.
Soybeans are usually ready to harvest 120-150 days after
planting, so you should be looking at February to March.

Signs your soybeans are ready:
- 95% of pods have turned brown
- Seeds rattle inside the pods when shaken
- Moisture content is below 13%

Don't wait too long - over-mature pods can shatter and
drop beans on the ground, which means lost yield!

Or even send a photo for diagnosis — the same vision AI that powers the web chat works through Telegram too.

Push Notifications: Advice That Comes to You

The real power of Telegram isn't the commands — it's the push notifications. When an extension officer logs a visit, the farmer's bot automatically sends them a summary:

Bot: New Visit Summary

Your extension officer John Smith visited your farm today.

Key observations:
- Maize is at V8 stage, generally healthy
- Fall armyworm detected in eastern 0.5ha section
- Soil moisture adequate after recent rains

Action items:
HIGH  Spray for armyworm within 48 hours
MED   Schedule AN top-dressing for next week
LOW   Consider mulching to conserve soil moisture

Full details available on your FarmMonitor dashboard.

This arrives on the farmer's phone as a regular Telegram message — no app to open, no website to load. It just appears, like a text from a friend.

The Technical Implementation

Each Telegram bot is created through Telegram's @BotFather. Each bot gets a unique token, which FarmMonitor stores securely in environment variables. When the server starts, it connects to all active bots and listens for messages:

// Bot initialization (simplified from botManager.ts)
export async function initializeBots() {
  const bots = await prisma.telegramBot.findMany({
    where: { isActive: true },
    include: { farmer: true }
  });

  for (const botRecord of bots) {
    const bot = new TelegramBot(botRecord.token, { polling: true });
    bot.on('message', (msg) => handleMessage(bot, botRecord, msg));
    activeBots.set(botRecord.id, bot);
  }
}

When a farmer sends a message to their bot, FarmMonitor identifies which farmer owns the bot, pulls their records, sends the message to Claude with full context, and returns the response through Telegram. The farmer never knows they're talking to an AI system — it just feels like messaging a really helpful agricultural advisor.

Channel 3: SMS (The True Last Mile)

Now for the farmers who don't have Telegram. Or smartphones. Or consistent data. They still have something: a phone number that can receive text messages.

SMS is the true last-mile channel. It's the technology equivalent of the postal service — not flashy, not feature-rich, but it reaches virtually everyone.

What Gets Sent via SMS

FarmMonitor doesn't try to replicate the full chat experience over SMS (160 characters doesn't leave much room for nuance). Instead, SMS is reserved for critical notifications:

  1. Visit summaries — A condensed version of the officer's findings
  2. Urgent recommendations — High-priority items that need immediate action
  3. Weather warnings — Frost alerts, extreme heat, or heavy rain incoming

Here's what an actual SMS looks like:

FarmMonitor: Visit summary from John Smith (15 Dec):
Armyworm detected in maize. URGENT: Spray Ampligo
within 48hrs. Top-dress AN at 200kg/ha next week.

That's 155 characters — just under the 160-character SMS limit. Every character counts.

How SMS Delivery Works

FarmMonitor uses Africa's Talking — a pan-African communications API that handles SMS delivery across the continent:

// SMS delivery via Africa's Talking (simplified)
import AfricasTalking from 'africastalking';

const at = AfricasTalking({
  username: process.env.AT_USERNAME,
  apiKey: process.env.AT_API_KEY
});

async function sendSms(phone: string, message: string) {
  const sms = at.SMS;
  const result = await sms.send({
    to: [phone],        // +263... format
    message: message,
    from: 'FarmMonitor'
  });
  // Log delivery status
  await prisma.smsLog.create({
    data: { phone, message, status: result.SMSMessageData.Recipients[0].status }
  });
}

The beauty is that it's fire-and-forget from the officer's perspective. They log one visit, and the system handles delivery across all three channels automatically.

The Three-Channel Strategy in Practice

Here's how a typical notification flows across all channels:

Officer logs visit for Munashe Mbizi
         |
         |--> Web App: Full visit details on dashboard
         |     (available when farmer opens the app)
         |
         |--> Telegram: @munashembizi_bot push notification
         |     (arrives instantly if farmer has Telegram)
         |
         +--> SMS: "Visit summary from John Smith..."
               (arrives even on basic phones)

The farmer might see all three, or just one — it doesn't matter. The goal is that the advice gets through, regardless of what technology the farmer has access to that day.

Why Not Just WhatsApp?

This is the question everyone asks. WhatsApp is hugely popular in Zimbabwe — why not use that instead of Telegram + SMS?

Three reasons:

  1. WhatsApp Business API costs money and requires approval — Telegram's bot API is free and instant
  2. WhatsApp doesn't allow automated bots the same way Telegram does (they have strict template message requirements)
  3. SMS reaches feature phones that can't run any messaging app

That said, WhatsApp integration would be a natural future addition. The architecture is channel-agnostic — adding WhatsApp would just be another notification path.

Farmer Control

Farmers aren't bombarded against their will. On their Profile page (or through their officer), they can:

  • Toggle SMS on or off (some farmers prefer to save on text charges)
  • Choose their language (English, Shona, or Ndebele)
  • Use Telegram as much or as little as they want

It's their farm, their data, their choice.

The Human Impact

Let's bring it back to why this matters.

A farmer named Munashe has 3 hectares of maize in Marondera. His extension officer visits in December and spots early fall armyworm damage. Without FarmMonitor:

  • Munashe might remember the advice... or might not
  • If he forgets, the armyworm spreads
  • By the time the officer visits again in February, half the crop could be gone
  • A family that expected to harvest 15 tonnes now gets 7

With FarmMonitor:

  • The visit is logged instantly
  • Munashe gets a Telegram message on his phone within seconds
  • His wife sees the SMS notification on her basic phone
  • Both are reminded: spray within 48 hours
  • A week later, the system follows up: have you completed the armyworm treatment?
  • The armyworm is caught early, the crop is saved

That's not a technology story. That's a food security story.
